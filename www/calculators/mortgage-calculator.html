Fixed rate mortgage – how does it work?

A fixed rate mortgage is the interest rate you pay to the mortgage provider and remains the same throughout the mortgage term typically lasting 2,3,5 or 10 years. The interest rate you pay is not affected by the lender’s standard variable rate or the Bank of England’s base rate. At the end of the fixed term, your mortgage will usually be transferred to the lenders standard variable rate (SVC) but this is usually higher than average so at this point you would likely find a new mortgage deal.

The key advantage of a fixed rate mortgage is that it you can be sure that there will be no changes in the interest rate you are paying. So for homeowners that are looking to budget, they will know exactly how much they will be paying month after month and year after year. If there are ever changed in the Bank of England’s base rate, you will not be affected because you will always be paying the same interest rate to your mortgage provider. There is also the opportunity to benefit from a lower interest rate in the first few years giving new buyers some disposable income to spend on furniture and home improvements.

Types of fixed rate mortgages

Fixed rate mortgage typically last for 2 to 3 years but lenders are most recently offering 5 to 10 year products. The longer the mortgage term, the higher the interest rate you usually pay. This is because there is a premium for the added security for paying a fixed rate over a long period of time, free of any market fluctuations to the variable rate or Bank of England base rate.

2 year fixed rate mortgage

A 2 year fix is a very low-risk option because you will benefit if the other variable or base rate increases but if it does not, you can always change your mortgage after the two-year spell.

3 year fixed rate mortgage

By taking a 3 year fixed rate mortgage, the typical interest rate is likely to be slightly higher than a 2 year fix because you are paying for the extra security. However, this product might suit various homeowners who plan to move house again in the next years. This way, they can budget exactly what they are going to spend and can plan ahead.

5 year fixed rate mortgage

Since a 5 year fixed rate mortgage is a long period, the homeowner may in fact lose out on better rates in the market. If their mortgage company’s variable rate is lower, the homeowner will not be able to change mortgages very easily. The fixed rate mortgages have an early repayment penalty in order to tie you in for longer and this can cost up to 6 months of mortgage payments.

10 year fixed rate mortgage

The 10-year fix is usually the most expensive because again, you are paying to avoid the chance of any rate fluctuations. Similar to the 5-year product, there are early payment penalties that apply should you wish to change the type of mortgage product. As a result, homeowners on a 10 year fixed mortgage rate may miss out on better rates available. Mortgage applicants are advised to consider the possibility of better rates being available before proceeding.

Useful advice

For anyone applying for a fixed rate mortgage, it is important to think about how long you want the loan for. If you think that you are in a financial position where you will be able to overpay on your mortgage, you can do so for up to 10% per year. However, there will be an early payment penalty should you wish to clear your debt early, up to 6 months worth of payments and this is not ideal. So it is essential to weigh up just how long you want the fixed mortgage for or whether you think that you would be better off with a tracker mortgage or variable mortgage.

If you are still undecided, one of the most common things to do with a fixed rate mortgage is to use the fixed rate for 2 years and then to assess the variable rates and Bank of England base rate. Here, you can decide whether you should switch to a different mortgage product or indeed take out a new 2 year mortgage.

When comparing the cost of mortgage products, it is important to remember that additional fees apply. Mortgage providers will always charge an arrangement fee for organizing the loan and this can cost a few hundred or thousand pounds. However, depending on the mortgage company, there may be deals available that reduce the arrangement fee e.g discounts for first time buyers. Also, be sure to see if the mortgage company offers rewards such as a cashback.

Finally, it is recommended to carefully budget how much you can afford to repay on your mortgage each month. Failing to repay your monthly repayment on time may incur further charges and long-term arrears may lead to the repossession of your property. There is also the negative impact on your credit rating because your credit file is shared between the mortgage lender and other credit reference agencies. Failing to repay your mortgage payments on time may cause your credit score to fall and make it harder to access credit in the future.
