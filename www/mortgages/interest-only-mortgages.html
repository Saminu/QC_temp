What is an interest only mortgage?

An interest only mortgage means you only pay the interest part of your mortgage and then you repay the capital at the end of the mortgage term (such as 25 years) in one big lump sum.

This is different to a typical mortgage repayment plan that consists of paying off the capital and interest together. The capital refers to the property or estate and the interest refers to the charges that you are paying to the lender. You will pay off the interest and capital together each month but in the early stages of the mortgage, you will pay back more interest than capital and then eventually you will be paying more capital than interest.

The advantages of interest only mortgages

The best thing about interest only mortgages is that it is the cheapest way to pay for a property in the short run. Since you are only paying the interest of the loan, you are paying small payments each month in return for owning a property. This approach is very popular for first time buyers who don’t have big savings and can only afford to pay small amounts each month and it allows them to enjoy the property for years to come at minimal cost.

The disadvantages of interest only mortgages

The downside of an interest only mortgage is that it does not actually reduce your debt because you will still be required to pay the capital at the end of the mortgage term. This leads to one hefty one lump sum repayment at the end of the mortgage period and borrowers are encouraged to put together an investment or savings plan in order to make the final repayment. The lender will typically need to assess your financial situation carefully and see evidence of a savings plan before proceeding.

Some consider the interest only mortgage to be quite risky because you are putting off repayment for a long time in the future. As a borrower, you will have to be responsible to keep saving money in order to fulfill the final repayment. Sadly, the number of interest only mortgaged being offered over the years has reduced significantly as financial institutions have raised concerns that consumers may not have repayment plans in place and therefore won’t be able to pay the capital at the end of the term.

Repayment Mortgages – The Alternative

A repayment mortgage is the alternative to an interest free mortgage and incorporates the traditional method of repaying off the interest and capital at the same time. Initially, you will pay more interest and less capital but as you rear towards the end of the mortgage term, you will be paying less interest by more capital.

Overall with a repayment mortgage you pay less interest than you would for an interest free mortgage because the capital is gradually reducing and the interest is not accruing as much.

Mortgages are portable meaning that they can be transferred to different products and repayment terms. One way to manage an interest only mortgage better is to pay as normal and once you are in a better financial situation, to switch to a repayment mortgage. By switching to a repayment mortgage, you will be able to pay off the capital as normal and avoid the large payment at the end of the mortgage term. Also, if you have plans to remortgage, you would have paid off more capital so you will be able to access a mortgage with a lower LTV and a lower interest rate.
